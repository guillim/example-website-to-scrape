The Journal

Occasional notes from the shop — new arrivals, book club minutes, things Margaret the cat has done.

What we read in May

June 2 · by Marit Sølberg

An unusually strong month, even by our usual quietly-enthusiastic standards. The Lantern Keeper's Daughter topped the till for the third consecutive week — a slow-burn debut from Elsie M. Penrose that has been winning over almost every customer who picks it up. (Jaya's pitch: “Like a Penelope Fitzgerald novel that fell in love with a lighthouse.”)

The translated fiction club discussed Léa Vermeulen's The Houseboat on the Saar, which produced the longest discussion in club history — two and a half hours, three pots of tea, and one minor disagreement about whether the narrator is reliable, unreliable, or just very tired. (We will, of course, reconvene next month.)

In the Nook, Theo has been reading Pip and the Great Big Pocket at story-time. Margaret the cat has been reading nothing at all.

Notes from the Nature Writing Book Club

May 24 · by Jaya Kapoor

Thirteen of us crammed into the back room on Wednesday to discuss Six Walks in Late Light by Hugo Ferreira. Six walks. Six new opinions. Five biscuits left.

The most-quoted line of the evening was, predictably, the one about “the small private grief of October.” The least-discussed chapter was the third, which Caroline gently described as “the bit where he just lists trees.”

Next month: Tomasz Brennan's A Field Guide to Quiet Places. Free, open to all. Bramble will be there.

A small note on special orders

May 18 · by Theo Chen

We've recently signed on with two new distributors which means our special-order turnaround has dropped from three weeks to roughly eight working days for UK titles. For US and continental Europe titles it's now about ten to fourteen days. If you'd like us to track something down, please drop into the shop or get in touch via the Contact page.

We've also expanded the antiquarian list — if you collect first editions, especially mid-century literary fiction, do come and talk to Marit.

Why we still write staff-pick cards by hand

May 9 · by Marit Sølberg

Every couple of weeks one of us picks a book and writes a short note about it, tucked inside the front cover. We've been doing this since 1994 — Maggie introduced it after reading about a New York shop doing the same — and there's no reason for us to stop.

A handwritten card is a small act of trust: this particular human, on a particular morning, with a particular cup of coffee, thought this particular book might find you. There is, we think, no algorithm that can do that for you. Although Elias keeps threatening to build one.

Welcome to our new Journal

May 1 · by The Inkwell & Quill

This is the very first entry in what we hope will be an occasional, unhurried record of life at the shop. New books, book-club minutes, oddities Elias has found in the stock-room, things Margaret the cat has done. Expect entries roughly once a fortnight. Expect the schedule to slip, gracefully.
